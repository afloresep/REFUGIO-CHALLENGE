Replay-policy result:

No candidate exceeded the current 1026 total.

Invalidated boundary probe:

An evaluator-boundary monkeypatch was tested and rejected. It is not a legal
routing policy under the current criterion because it replaces evaluator code
instead of returning warehouse actions from `act()`. It is therefore excluded
from `data/evaluation-results.json` and from valid score claims. With that
criterion, the active fallback target is 1040 and the verified legal floor
remains `1026 = 345, 342, 339`.

Findings:

- The naive full-day fixed-priority scheduler is valid but far too
  conservative: seed0 scored 298 with `deliveries_desc` order, versus the
  current replay matrix's 345. Static whole-day priority starves late robots
  and is not competitive with the live planner's cooperative traffic pattern.
- Re-running single-robot compression from the exact 1026 seed0 matrix found no
  further +1. Robot 37 can still reach 5 deliveries, but the total remains 345
  because robot 17 falls from 4 to 3.
- The robot 17 / robot 37 trade is a real shelf-lock exclusion. Both robots need
  shelf `(24, 7)` as their next extra target. Robot 37's fifth-trip pickup at
  tick 250 locks the shelf until its tick-297 drop, blocking robot 17's tick-256
  pickup. Delaying robot 37 until robot 17 releases the shelf leaves too little
  time for robot 37 to return to its bottom base.
- Seed0 has 36 neutral same-delivery compression edits, but staging each before
  robot 37's extra trip still gives exactly the same trade: gain robot 37, lose
  robot 17, net 0.
- Lock-floor repair did not produce a net improvement on any seed.
- Targeted neutral-plus-near-miss pair searches found no wins for seed1 robots
  38/57 or seed2 robot 41.
- Loss-set repair found no wins. Seed0's self-gaining rows were:
  - robot 37: total 345, gain robot 37, lose robot 17
  - robot 92: total 343, gain robot 92, lose robots 42, 78, 93
  - robot 93: total 342, gain robot 93, lose robots 89, 94
  Rebuilding the damaged robots did not lift any case above the 345 seed0
  floor.
- Random neutral-row LNS also stayed flat: seed0 best 345 after 900 exact
  evaluations, seed1 best 342 after 350, seed2 best 339 after 350.
- Suffix joint replanning did not produce a score improvement:
  - robot 17/37 cannot both complete the extra shelf `(24, 7)` from tick 230
  - robot 92's loss set improved the whole-row replacement case from 343 to
    344 but never exceeded the 345 seed0 floor; robot 42 remained the hard
    casualty for starts 160..240, while start 250 preserved the incumbent with
    no gain
  - robot 93's set never exceeded the incumbent; starts 140..220 preserved
    345 but produced no extra delivery
  - seed1 and seed2 single-robot suffix attempts preserved the incumbent
    scores but did not increase the target robots' delivery counts
- Targeted suffix sweeps also stayed flat:
  - seed1: 111 feasible records, 0 net wins, 0 self-gains, best 342
  - seed2: 47 feasible records, 0 net wins, 0 self-gains, best 339
- The newly fetched public jobs are useful as negative controls but not as
  candidate parents for 1040. The public 930 jobs evaluate locally at
  `306, 318, 306`; the public 931 job evaluates at `307, 318, 306`; the
  public 925 job evaluates at `307, 315, 303`.
- Randomized suffix LNS did not open a path to 1040. Larger neighborhoods were
  usually infeasible when asked to preserve each moving robot's incumbent
  delivery count. Smaller feasible neighborhoods preserved or lost score but
  did not add a delivery.
- The trip-by-trip schedulers are valid diagnostics but not candidates. They
  lose too much of the incumbent choreography, especially around idle robots
  blocking base entries and late suffix coupling.
- The 1026 replay's blocked moves are not a generic source of headroom. They
  are mostly symptoms of the same non-composing seed0 shelf-lock trade already
  seen in the robot 17/37 and robot 92/93 experiments.
- A fresh 45-trial live-policy boost grind found no single-boost improvement.

Interpretation:

The cheap local replay edits that produced 1025 and 1026 are now exhausted
under the tested one- and two-robot neighborhoods. The remaining gap to the
legal 1040 fallback target is not likely to come from isolated row compression.
It needs a broader multi-robot rescheduler that preserves the good cooperative
traffic pattern while changing shelf-lock order, or a different layout/planner
family whose replay matrices start closer to the uncongested model. The new
greedy trip schedulers show that simply interleaving trips is not enough: the
incumbent value is in detailed corridor timing, not just target ordering.

Next:

- Build a replay optimizer that can replan a small set of robots jointly with
  explicit shelf-lock ordering, not just one robot against frozen traffic.
- Use current replay conflicts to choose joint neighborhoods around shared
  target shelves and base corridors.
- Treat the naive full-day scheduler as a diagnostic baseline only; it should
  not be used as a candidate generator until it can preserve more of the
  incumbent traffic flow.
